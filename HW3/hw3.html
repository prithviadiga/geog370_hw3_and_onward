To display the world map in all of the different projections below, I imported the provided shapefile to QGIS, and in the lower right corner, changed the projections to my liking. I then downloaded and installed the indicatrix mapper, and then clicked on the indicatrix icon on the top bar and enabled it, which created a layer of lined circles at different latitudes on the map to show the relative size of that specific latitude. After getting this layer, I formatted the map with descriptive text and then uploaded it to my local files as a png file.

WGS84

WGS84 stretches areas near to the poles and increases their relative size in order to allow for grid-like coordinates. In order to ensure that landmasses
at extreme latitudes don't become too big, they are then squished so as to minimize the size increase in the eyes of the viewer.

Aitoff Projection

The Aitoff projection is an oval projection, abandoning the grid system of coordinates. It is better at preserving size than WGS84 but can cause
landmasses towards the edge of the projection to become distorted. An example of this can be seen in Canada and Alaska. This issue is somewhat
solved since most of the area towards the edges of the map are ocean.

Pseudo-Mercator Projection

Pseudo-Mercator Projection projects the spherical contents of the globe onto an unrolled cylindrical surface in order to preserve a grid-like layout.
this causes areas near the poles to be expanded in size relative to their actual size, while keeping areas close to the equator to maintian their size.

Winkel I

Winkel 1 consists of two straight lines, the Equator and the Prime Meridian. All other meridians are curved away from the Prime. While still
causing expansion at the poles, it is not as defined as others such as Mercator. There is significant bulging towards the equator but this is remedied
by the fact that this distorsion is mainly caused over ocean.

Cylindrical Equal-Area Projection

The Cylindrical Equal-Area Projection maintains a rectangular shape and square coordinates and also maintains relative areas on a map. This is
achieved by stretching areas closer to the poles to match the size of the equator. Areas remain the size they should be but their appearence is distorted.

Equidistant Conic Projection

Equidistant Conic Projections involve a concentric circle and arc pair acting as the bounds for straight lines of longitude that meet at the North pole and
diverge at the South. This creates a conical shape. Shape is distorted and areas further out from the center are larger.

Azimuthal Equidistant Projection

Azimuthal Equidistant Projection consist of straight longitudinal lines projecting from a center point. The areas of the earth furthest away from the center
point are heavily distorted while areas closer retain scale as well as distance. Along latitudes the scale, distance, and distorsion are not relevant but when
compared to areas of different longitudes it becomes a problem. This map generally works when the scale of areas furthest from the center doesn't
matter (oceans/ Antarctica).
